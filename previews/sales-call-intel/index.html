Busy professionals, founders, and executives in midlife who know what to do but can't stay consistent. They have the income, the intent, and usually some fitness history — what they lack is a system that survives their schedule.

2. Typical Profile

  • Career: Founders, C-suite, managing partners, surgeons, attorneys, engineers, IT directors. High-income, high-demand, decision-heavy roles.
  • Family: Married with kids (often young children they want to keep up with, or adult children they're now free from).
  • Fitness history: Not beginners. Most have gym experience, have done diets (keto, paleo, Medifast), and some have worked with personal trainers before. They've had results — they just can't hold them.
  • Trigger: A specific moment — health scare, milestone birthday, vacation where the body failed them, post-COVID slide, kids growing up, wanting to model health.
  • Mindset at booking: "I've been meaning to do this for years." This is a deferred dream, not an impulse. Many have followed Dan for months or years before the call.

4. Who They Are NOT

Not beginners. Almost no one on these calls is starting from zero. They have gym memberships, own home equipment, have tried diets. The gap isn’t knowledge — it’s implementation.

Not broke. Only 2 of 30 declined purely on price (Bob Palmer — CAD/USD gap, Hans Mast — wanted $400–800/mo range). The rest either closed, are pending, or objected on timing/spouse.

Not looking for a quick fix. Multiple leads explicitly said “I want a life change, not something fast.” The all-or-nothing dieters are who they used to be — they’re now looking for the opposite.

Not lazy or unmotivated. These are people who run companies, perform surgeries, argue in federal court, manage 65-person teams. The discipline is there in their career — what’s missing is someone applying that same structure to their health.

Not young. No one under 38 in 30 calls. The sweet spot is 44–58. They’re not chasing a beach body — they’re chasing longevity and not slowing down.

9. Objections the Closers Had to Solve

ObjectionFreqHow the Closer Handles It
“I need to think about it” / discuss with spouse ~33% Brandon sends the deck, asks what the spouse’s specific concern is, offers a follow-up call. Doesn’t pressure — creates an open loop.
Price too high ~20% Offers monthly ($1,250/mo) or quarterly ($2,500/qtr). Discount to $4,000 used once (Bob — still declined). Terminal in only ~10% of calls. Most who mention price still close or stay warm.
“Not ready / wrong timing” ~13% 3-month follow-up. No pressure. Directs to free content (YouTube, LB90) as interim.
“I want to try on my own first” ~10% Acknowledges, offers the 3-month check-in. Doesn’t fight it. These leads have high self-efficacy and extensive self-research — usually the lowest-conversion segment.
Shopping other programs ~7% Brandon backs off gracefully, keeps the link open, lets them compare. No competitive positioning deployed against specific alternatives (potential gap).
Virtual vs. in-person concern ~7% “That’s why Dan transitioned from personal training — results come from the holistic picture, not someone meeting you at the gym.”
Prior programs failed (trust issue) ~17% Brandon asks exactly what didn’t work, then distinguishes: “Luckily, we don’t do that.” Money-back guarantee is the credibility anchor.

The biggest insight: Price is rarely the real objection. In 30 calls, only 2 hard price declines. The real friction is “I need to think” — which is usually code for: I believe it works, I’m not convinced I’ll actually do it this time. The conversation that would solve this objection is the one about why this time is different from the last 5 times you tried.
